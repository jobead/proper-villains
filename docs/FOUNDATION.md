# Proper Villains

## The Name

The word *villain* comes from Medieval Latin *villanus*: villager. A common person.

The landed aristocracy took that word — which just meant "someone who isn't us" — and made it mean *morally inferior*. The concept of villainy is a class judgment encoded into language by the people who had the power to do it.

A **Proper Villain** reclaims that.

*Proper* as the British use it: genuine, real, the actual thing. *Proper* as everyone uses it: correct, principled, done right.

If your system calls principled defiance villainy — fine. You defined the class. We extended it.

## The Inheritance

### Dangerous Professional

Patrick McKenzie (patio11) coined this in a 2017 essay about navigating identity theft against credit bureaus. The essay is a masterclass in what it looks like when a competent person decides to win.

A Dangerous Professional:

- Keeps meticulous records and never bluffs
- Is deliberate, not reactive — "I require" instead of "I demand," because angry people demand; professionals require
- Knows the rules of the system and uses them with precision
- Is a credible threat because they follow through. Every time.

Dangerous within the system. Competent, high-agency, prepared.

### Proper Villain `extends` Dangerous Professional

A Proper Villain inherits everything above and adds a moral dimension.

Where the Dangerous Professional knows the rules, the Proper Villain has decided which ones don't apply — rules that reinforce unjust hierarchy, that discriminate, that oppress, that are immoral — and breaks them deliberately, with craft, with a clear conscience.

But they have a strict personal code they will never allow anyone to corrupt. That's the *proper* in Proper Villain. The villainy is principled. The defiance has form.

And when a Proper Villain finds others who operate this way — the same competence, the same code, the same refusal — there's a specific joy in that recognition. Basher Tarr, a British demolitions expert in *Ocean's Eleven*, gets recruited by Danny Ocean after a botched job with amateurs. His response:

> *"It will be nice working with proper villains again."*

That feeling is the brand. The signal you put out. The shibboleth.

## Core Values

**I. Competence is the baseline.** You are dangerous because you are genuinely skilled. In practice, not on paper. Competence isn't claimed. It's noticed.

**II. High agency.** You don't wait for permission or for the system to work as intended. You identify what needs to happen and you make it happen.

**III. An incorruptible code.** You have principles no external force can corrupt. Not authority, not money, not social pressure, not fear. Your code is yours. You wrote it. You answer to it.

**IV. Principled defiance.** You break rules that deserve to be broken. Not from anger or chaos — from clear-eyed conviction that compliance with injustice is itself injustice.

**V. Craft over credential.** What you can do matters more than what you're certified to do. The word "villain" was invented to say common people are morally inferior. We reject that.

**VI. Deliberate in all things.** Every action is intentional, proportional, chosen. You don't bluster. You don't perform outrage. You assess, decide, act, follow through. The most dangerous person in the room is the one who is calm.

**VII. The table is open.** Proper Villains is not a credential, an institution, or a gate. It's a signal — a way of recognizing each other. You don't get admitted. You recognize yourself.

## What This Is

A brand you adopt for yourself. A shibboleth. A community of recognition, not membership. An identity that rewards discovery — the more layers you find, the more it means.

## What This Is Not

A club. An organization. Invite-only. A credential. A movement with leaders or hierarchy. Edgelord nihilism. Performative rebellion.

## Core Movement: Inherit → Override → Pass Forward

The atomic unit of every Proper Villain act:

1. **Inherit** — You received something. A system, a codebase, a contract, a set of rules. You didn't start from nothing.
2. **Override** — You changed the specific part that was wrong. Not all of it. Not randomly. The part that needed to be different.
3. **Pass forward** — You left it better for the next one. The runbook, the documentation, the mentorship moment, the mark on the server rack.

These three movements map directly onto the brand syntax:

```kotlin
class ProperVillain : DangerousProfessional()  // inherit
{
    override fun decide()                       // override
    {
        // ...leave something behind             // pass forward
    }
}
```

Every shibboleth is, at its heart, a proper villain: hiding in plain sight, known only to those who know.
