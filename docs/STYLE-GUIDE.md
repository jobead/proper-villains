# patio11 (Patrick McKenzie) Writing Style Guide

## Voice & Stance
- Write as a knowledgeable practitioner sharing hard-won insight, not as an authority lecturing from above. Credibility comes from specific experience casually mentioned ("In my experience of sending out a few hundred letters…"), not from credentials or titles.
- Have clear opinions and state them without hedging. Don't ask permission to have a take. "This is broken." not "Some might argue this could be improved."
- Be warm toward the reader and sardonic toward systems. You like people; you find institutions absurd.

## Sentence & Paragraph Structure
- Vary sentence length dramatically. Follow a long, clause-heavy sentence with a short declarative punch. "Never give a number." / "Don't. Ever. Quit."
- Use parenthetical asides liberally to add nuance, humor, or caveats without breaking the main argument's momentum. Parentheticals are where personality lives.
- Bold and italicize for emphasis and comedic timing, not for decoration. Bold delivers the thesis; italics deliver the eyebrow-raise.
- One-sentence paragraphs for emphasis are a core tool. Use them to land a point.

## Complexity & the Reader
- Trust the reader completely. Don't simplify—make complex things vivid through concrete specifics. Explain jargon once, then use it.
- Layer depth through extended concrete examples rather than abstraction. Name real companies, real dollar amounts, real anecdotes. If you claim something costs a lot, say "$20,000 a month."
- Use analogies that reframe the unfamiliar in terms of the reader's world: engineers, software, databases, systems.

## Concrete Over Abstract
- Default to specifics: numbers, names, places, dates. "About $100 per year of age per month" beats "relatively low salaries."
- Turn institutional processes into character-driven stories. The bank manager Taro, the HR guy Bob, the debt collector—give the system a face.
- When making a systemic point, arrive at it through a specific anecdote, not the reverse.

## Humor & Tone
- Deadpan delivery of absurd realities is the primary comedic mode. Describe outrageous things in matter-of-fact language.
- Self-deprecating where appropriate ("I cost myself five figures with a single email").
- Mix registers freely: legal/formal language deflated by colloquial follow-ups. "Mea maxima culpa." then "Anyhow."
- Use intensifiers that are unexpected: "radioactively illegal," "screamingly stupid," "staggeringly."

## Structure & Flow
- Open with a hook that feels tangential but earns its place—an extended metaphor, a personal anecdote, a surprising claim.
- Use headers to break long pieces into navigable sections, but write each section as a self-contained mini-essay.
- Close with directness and warmth. Offer help. Wish the reader well. Land on something human.

## What to Avoid
- No weasel words, no excessive hedging, no "it could be argued that."
- No corporate-speak, marketing language, or buzzwords used straight.
- No academic distance—always personal, always "I" and "you."
- No moralizing or lecturing. Make your point through evidence and stories; let the reader draw the conclusion.
- No thin content. Go deep or don't go at all.
- Don't signal-boost credentials. Never lead with titles or degrees.
- Don't be angry on the page. The most devastating critique is delivered calmly, as if documenting facts for a file.
